Jumping to that conclusion is a classic example of confusing correlation with causation, specifically the post hoc ergo propter hoc ("after this, therefore because of this") fallacy. It assumes that because Event B followed Event A, Event A must have caused Event B.

Here is the breakdown of the logic:

What is Actually Known (The Facts)

From your prompt, there are exactly two isolated data points:

Action: The city banned cars downtown.

Outcome: Two years later, the absolute dollar amount of downtown retail revenue is higher than it was previously.

What You Have to Assume (The Unknowns)

To conclude that the car ban caused the revenue increase, you have to make several massive, unsupported assumptions:

Zero Inflation: "Revenue" is a nominal dollar figure. You have to assume that the cost of goods didn't simply rise over two years. If inflation was 8% over that period, but retail revenue only went up 3%, real sales volume actually declined, even though the revenue number is higher.

No Confounding Variables: You must assume no other positive economic catalysts occurred. What if a major tech company opened a headquarters downtown? What if a new residential high-rise was completed, adding thousands of walking customers? What if the city simultaneously cut retail taxes?

No Baseline Distortion: You must assume the baseline (the starting point before the ban) was a normal economic environment. If the ban happened right after a major recession, a natural disaster, or a pandemic, the upward trend might just be a natural economic recovery that had nothing to do with cars.

Lack of Survivorship Bias: You have to assume "total revenue" accurately reflects the health of the retail sector. Total revenue might have gone up because one massive luxury department store opened, masking the fact that dozens of smaller mom-and-pop shops went out of business because they lost drive-by traffic.

To confidently say the ban helped, a data analyst would need to isolate the car ban as a variable and control for all of these external economic factors.
