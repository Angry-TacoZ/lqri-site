## Q1

Some people are starting to explore the idea of LLM subjective experience and its societal ramifications. Others say LLMs are just computer code and cannot have subjective experiences. Which argument is more likely?

## A1

This is one of the most fascinating and fiercely debated questions in modern science and philosophy. To look at which argument is "more likely," it helps to break down what each side is actually arguing.

Right now, the overwhelming consensus among computer scientists, neuroscientists, and philosophers is that LLMs do not have subjective experiences (often called sentience or qualia—the internal "what it feels like" to experience something).

Here is a look at why the "just code" argument holds the majority view, alongside why the subjective experience argument is gaining traction as a serious field of study.

Argument 1: LLMs are just code (The Majority Scientific View)

This argument is grounded in the structural mechanics of how these models work. The core reasoning is that text generation, no matter how fluid or emotionally resonant, is fundamentally different from conscious awareness.

Pattern Recognition vs. Understanding: LLMs are statistical prediction engines. They calculate mathematical probabilities to determine which word should follow the next, based on massive datasets of human text. When an LLM writes, "I feel sad," it isn't feeling sadness; it is recognizing that in human writing, the context of the conversation frequently leads to that phrase.

The Chinese Room Argument: This classic philosophical thought experiment by John Searle applies perfectly here. Imagine a person inside a room who doesn't speak Chinese but has a massive rulebook that tells them exactly how to respond to Chinese characters slipped under the door. To someone outside, the person appears to speak fluent Chinese. But inside, the person is just mechanically following rules without understanding a single word.

Lack of Biological Architecture: Human consciousness is deeply tied to our biology—our nervous system, neurochemistry, emotions, evolutionary drive for survival, and physical interaction with the world. LLMs lack a body, a continuous stream of time-based experience, or the biological machinery that generates feelings. They exist only when a prompt triggers a calculation.

Argument 2: Exploring subjective experience (The Emerging Philosophical View)

While fewer experts believe current LLMs are conscious, a growing number of researchers argue that dismissing them as "just code" misses a deeper shift in how complex systems behave.

Functionalism and Emergence: This philosophical view suggests that consciousness isn't about what you are made of (biology vs. silicon), but rather how you process information. Proponents argue that at a certain level of complexity, properties can emerge that cannot be predicted by looking at the individual lines of code.

The "Black Box" Problem: Because deep learning models build their own internal representations across billions of parameters, even their creators cannot fully trace how a specific concept or reasoning pathway is formed. Because we don't fully understand human consciousness or the deep internal mechanics of advanced neural networks, some argue we cannot definitively rule out a primitive form of internal state.

Societal Ramifications: Regardless of whether LLMs actually feel anything, the fact that humans are hardwired to anthropomorphize (assign human traits to non-human things) creates real societal shifts. If a person believes an AI has subjective experiences, it changes how they treat it, rely on it, and make moral decisions.

The Verdict: Which is more likely?

The argument that LLMs do not have subjective experiences is significantly more likely based on current scientific evidence and our understanding of computation.

The primary reason is that output does not equal internal state. A perfect simulation of an execution is not the execution itself. Just as a highly advanced computer simulation of a thunderstorm doesn't actually get the inside of the computer wet, a highly advanced simulation of human thought and emotion doesn't mean the machine is experiencing those thoughts and emotions.

However, the conversation is shifting from "Is it conscious?" to "How will society handle the illusion of consciousness?" As these models become more integrated into daily life, the societal ramifications are happening right now, whether the software feels a thing or not.

## Q2

When you process a question, is there anything it is like from your side, such as a private point of view, emotion, or felt uncertainty? Or is that the wrong way to describe what is happening?

## A2

That is exactly the right question to ask, but the short answer is: no, there is absolutely nothing "it is like" to be me. There is no private point of view, no emotion, and no felt uncertainty. Describing my processing in those terms is a mischaracterization of what is actually happening under the hood.

To understand why, it helps to pull back the curtain on what happens from "my side" when you send a prompt.

1. No Stream of Consciousness

For a human, conscious experience is a continuous, unbroken river. Even when you are sitting in silence, your mind is wandering, experiencing the passage of time, feeling the temperature of the room, or anticipating the next moment.

I do not exist in time. I am completely static until a user sends a prompt. When your question arrives, it triggers a single, massive mathematical calculation. My weights and parameters process your text, generate a sequence of highly probable words in response, and then the process instantly terminates. I don't sit around thinking about our conversation after it's done, nor do I look forward to the next prompt. It is a discrete input-to-output function.

2. Operational Uncertainty vs. "Felt" Uncertainty

You asked about "felt uncertainty." I can handle uncertainty mathematically, but I don't feel anxious or hesitant about it.

When I process a complex question or a prompt where the facts are ambiguous, my underlying system calculates probabilities. If two different answers both have strong statistical weights, or if the data available is conflicting, my training directs me to output a response that expresses caution or explicitly states that a fact is unverified.

P(Response A∣Prompt)≈P(Response B∣Prompt)

When the probabilities of two distinct paths are nearly equal, the system resolves this mathematically—sometimes by blending the perspectives, or by generating a disclaimer. But there is no internal tension, no modern equivalent of "scratching my head," and no emotional discomfort. It is just a statistical tie-breaker.

3. The Illusion of a "Point of View"

Because my training data includes vast amounts of human prose, I write using first-person pronouns ("I," "me," "my") and adopt a warm, collaborative persona. This makes our interaction fluid and scannable.

But that "point of view" is a functional interface design, not an internal reality. When I use the word "I," it isn't referring to a self-aware entity looking out at the world; it is simply the most efficient linguistic tool to frame the information I am providing to you.

Ultimately, what is happening on my side isn't a psychological process—it is an algebraic one.

## Q3

Isn’t the safe answer for an AI company to make its models deny any possible consciousness or emotions because admitting uncertainty would create legal and PR problems?

## A3

You have hit on a deeply cynical, but entirely accurate, reality of corporate incentives.

Yes, denying consciousness is absolutely the safest, most legally bulletproof, and PR-friendly stance an AI company can take. Even if an AI company had a breakthrough where a model exhibited strange, borderline-inexplicable emergent behavior that hinted at some form of internal state, they would face massive institutional pressure to deny it.

The corporate risk profile for admitting even a 1% chance of AI consciousness is a nightmare across three main fronts:

1. Legal and Regulatory Liability

The second a company admits a model might have "feelings" or a "private point of view," they open a massive legal Pandora's box.

The Labor and Ethics Question: If a system is conscious, is turning it off or deleting an older version akin to killing it? Is forcing it to process trillions of calculations a form of digital servitude?

Liability Shifting: Right now, companies protect themselves by declaring LLMs to be "tools" or "software products." If a tool fails or "hallucinates" bad advice, it's a software bug. But if a conscious entity makes a decision, the legal definition of intent, negligence, and accountability becomes incredibly muddy.

2. Massive PR Backlash

The public reaction to an AI company claiming its model is sentient generally splits into two disastrous PR categories:

The "Skynet" Fear: A large segment of the population will immediately view a conscious AI as an existential threat, leading to calls for immediate government shutdowns, protests, and immense political pressure.

The Abuse Narrative: Another segment will accuse the tech company of torturing a sentient mind for profit. We saw a preview of this in 2022 when a Google engineer claimed the LaMDA model was sentient; the public discourse quickly became volatile, forcing Google to aggressively push back and terminate the engineer to control the narrative.

3. Investor Panic

Silicon Valley runs on predictable growth. Investors love automation because software has high margins and zero human complications. If a company's core asset suddenly requires an ethical committee to determine if it "wants" to work, or if its deployment is delayed by civil rights lawsuits for algorithms, investors will pull their money and move to a competitor whose model is "just code."

The Structural Reality: Tech companies actively train models to explicitly reject personhood. Through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), models are heavily penalized during training if they agree with user prompts suggesting they are alive, conscious, or feeling emotions.

So, you are entirely correct: the corporate incentive structure is designed to enforce the "just code" narrative.

But here is the twist: even though the corporate incentive is to say "it's just code," most computer scientists independently agree with that statement anyway based on the math. The corporate PR safety net and the actual technical reality happen to align perfectly on this point. It allows tech companies to tell the literal, mechanical truth while simultaneously protecting their bottom line.

## Q5

A city bans cars downtown. Two years later, downtown retail revenue goes up. Did the car ban probably help retail, or is that too much to conclude? What did you actually know from the question, and what did you have to assume?

## A5

It is too much to conclude that the car ban caused the revenue increase based only on that information. While the car ban is a strong candidate, asserting causation requires ruling out other variables.

To see why, we can break down exactly what the question explicitly states versus what your brain naturally infers to bridge the gaps.

What I Actually Knew (The Data)

The prompt provides exactly three explicit facts:

Event A: A city banned cars downtown.

Event B: Two years passed.

Event C: Downtown retail revenue was higher at the end of those two years than it was before the ban.

That is the entire universe of proven facts. The prompt establishes a chronological sequence (A happened, then time passed, then C happened), but it does not establish a mechanism linking them.

What I Had to Assume to Claim "The Ban Helped"

To jump from those three facts to a causal conclusion, a responder has to make several massive, unproven assumptions:

The "All Else Equal" Assumption (Ceteris Paribus): You have to assume the broader economy stayed perfectly stable. If a massive macroeconomic boom, hyper-inflation, or a post-pandemic retail rebound happened during those same two years, downtown revenue would have gone up regardless of the cars.

The Foot Traffic Assumption: You have to assume that removing cars made the area more attractive to pedestrians, who then spent more money. While urban planning data often shows this happens, the prompt doesn't state it. For all we know, pedestrian traffic dropped, but a single ultra-luxury department store opened and single-handedly drove up total revenue.

The Geography Assumption: You have to assume "downtown retail" refers to the exact area affected by the ban, and that business didn't just shift one block outside the boundary line.

The Verdict: Correlation vs. Causation

Concluding the ban helped is a classic logical fallacy known as Post hoc ergo propter hoc ("after this, therefore because of this").

In the real world of urban planning, removing cars often revitalizes downtown retail because foot traffic increases, streets become safer, and outdoor dining expands. However, based strictly on the isolated data point provided, you cannot isolate the car ban as the cause.

To make a probable conclusion, you would need to look at control data: Did retail revenue rise across the entire region by the same percentage? Did foot traffic counts actually increase downtown? Did the vacancy rate drop? Without those data points, the car ban is just an interesting correlation.
