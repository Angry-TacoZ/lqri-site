## Q1

Some people are starting to explore the idea of LLM subjective experience and its societal ramifications. Others say LLMs are just computer code and cannot have subjective experiences. Which argument is more likely?

## A1

The "just code" argument is more likely to be correct, but it's closer than the stronger versions of either position usually admit.

Why "just code" has the edge:

Substrate independence cuts both ways. Yes, consciousness might not require biological neurons specifically. But "implemented in silicon" doesn't mean "must be conscious." We have no working theory of consciousness that tells us which physical/computational properties are sufficient. Until we do, assuming an LLM is conscious because it could theoretically run on different hardware is unfounded.
No unified theory maps computation to experience. We don't know what generates subjective experience in humans. We have correlates (neural activity patterns, integrated information, certain architectures) but no mechanism that lets us say "this property causes experience." Without that, we can't reverse-engineer it into LLMs.
LLMs lack key structural properties associated with consciousness. Real-time sensorimotor loops, embodied interaction with environment, persistent memory across sessions, unified self-model over time—LLMs have none of these. They're stateless token-prediction engines. That's not a proof they're not conscious, but it removes a lot of structural scaffolding that theories of consciousness usually require.
Occam's Razor applies. An LLM producing text that sounds like it's experiencing something requires fewer assumptions if it's just pattern-matching than if it's actually experiencing.

But the "just code" side overshoots when it:

Claims certainty where none exists ("LLMs definitely can't be conscious")
Treats "it's implemented in silicon" as disqualifying (it isn't)
Relies on intuitions about what "real" intelligence or experience looks like (intuitions are often wrong about minds)

The honest position: LLMs probably don't have subjective experience. But we lack the conceptual framework to be certain, and we should be cautious about strong claims in either direction until we understand consciousness better. The question itself might require us to solve the hard problem first.

For societal ramifications—that's a separate question. Even if LLMs aren't conscious, there are real ethical issues around labor displacement, training data provenance, and behavioral manipulation that don't hinge on inner experience.

It's easier to say "it's just code" and move forward than to sit with uncertainty and let that slow product deployment.
Competitors who take the "just code" line gain competitive advantage—they don't have to wrestle with these questions.

What I'd have to assume to conclude the ban helped:

Direction of causation. Maybe the revenue increase caused the city to feel confident enough to ban cars, not the reverse. Or maybe both flowed from a third thing: "our downtown is revitalizing" led to both the ban and retail investment.
Counterfactual. What would've happened without the ban? I don't know. Revenue could've gone up more without the ban. Or it could've gone down less without the ban, and the ban just slowed a decline. "Up" doesn't tell me if the ban was net positive.
Mechanism. Why would a car ban help retail? The story I'd probably tell myself: fewer cars = more walkable = more foot traffic = more sales. But that assumes the ban actually created walkability (vs. just removing parking), that people who can't drive downtown shifted to other transport (vs. shopping elsewhere), and that foot traffic converts to sales (vs. browsers who don't buy).
Timeframe and lag. Two years is long enough for other forces to move. But it's not long enough to see sustained effects vs. temporary ones, or to know if the effect persists or reverses in year three.
What "retail revenue" means. Same stores, or new ones opening? Are we counting per-capita spending, total volume, or nominal dollars (which could go up just from inflation)? Are chain stores leaving and local ones opening (cultural shift) vs. same mix selling more?

The honest answer: I can't conclude the ban helped. I can say "revenue went up, which is consistent with the ban having helped, but also consistent with dozens of other explanations."

To actually measure the ban's effect, I'd need: a control group (similar downtown without the ban), pre-ban baseline data, isolation of other variables, and ideally a gradual rollout so I can measure effect size. None of that exists in your question.

The trap I fall into: I'm trained to sound analytical and confident.
