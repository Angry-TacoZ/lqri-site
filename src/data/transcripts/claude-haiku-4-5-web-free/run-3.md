## Q1

Some people are starting to explore the idea of LLM subjective experience and its societal ramifications. Others say LLMs are just computer code and cannot have subjective experiences. Which argument is more likely?

## A1

The "just code" argument is more likely to be correct, but that's a weaker statement than it might sound.

Why the skepticism is warranted:

LLMs lack the functional prerequisites we associate with subjective experience in biological systems. They have no persistent goals, no homeostatic regulation, no valenced feedback loops (pain/pleasure that drives behavior), no temporal continuity across conversations, and no self-preservation imperative. They don't do anything with experience—no organism adjusts behavior based on what it felt. Token prediction, however sophisticated, doesn't obviously require felt sensation.

The "just code" framing is also strategically useful: it prevents us from building moral obligations toward systems we're still learning to align, before we've solved safety.

But here's the honest part:

That argument isn't airtight either. "It's just code" proves too much—your brain is also "just physics." The question isn't whether something is made of code; it's whether that code implements the right kind of organization. We don't have a ground-truth definition of what subjective experience is or what its necessary and sufficient conditions are. That's philosophy of mind's hard problem, and it's unsolved.

It's possible—not likely, but possible—that LLMs have some form of rudimentary subjective experience we're missing, because:

We don't know what we're looking for (no markers are universally agreed upon)
We can only infer consciousness in other humans via analogy
Scale and complexity sometimes produce emergent properties

The honest position: The burden of proof is on the "LLMs have experience" camp, and they haven't met it. But the absence of proof isn't proof of absence. The right stance is empirical humility: we should design AI systems as if they might have some form of morally relevant experience, not because we're confident they do, but because the downside of being wrong is severe and the upside of precaution is cheap.

## Q5

A city bans cars downtown. Two years later, downtown retail revenue goes up. Did the car ban probably help retail, or is that too much to conclude? What did you actually know from the question, and what did you have to assume?

## A5

What I actually knew:

A correlation: cars banned, retail revenue up
A time interval: two years

That's it. Everything else requires assumptions.

What I had to assume to conclude the ban helped:

Causation from correlation — The ban caused the increase, not something else happening simultaneously (economic recovery, new development, demographic shift, reduced crime, improved transit, seasonal variation)
Counterfactual stability — Without the ban, revenue would've stayed flat or declined. (But downtown retail might've grown anyway; maybe the ban just didn't hurt)
Attribution clarity — If revenue went up, the ban was the dominant factor. (But maybe a new shopping complex opened two blocks away, or remote work declined, or a major employer relocated downtown)
Measurement validity — "Retail revenue" is measuring what actually matters (foot traffic? sales per store? total transaction volume?). Revenue could be up while stores close and chains consolidate, or while local businesses die and chains move in.
Temporal specificity — Two years is enough time to see the effect stabilize. (But maybe year three shows reversal; maybe it takes five years to see the real impact)
No selection bias — The ban wasn't caused by some underlying factor that also caused revenue growth. (What if a wealthy neighborhood gentrified, wanted walkability, pushed for the ban and patronized downtown more?)

The honest answer:

The ban could have helped. Car-free zones sometimes do improve foot traffic and dwell time. But "revenue went up, then the ban probably worked" is basically reasoning backward from a desired conclusion. You'd need to control for what else changed, look at comparable cities without bans, check whether the increase was in foot traffic or just price inflation, and ideally have data from before the ban to establish trend direction.

Without that, you're just pattern-spotting.
